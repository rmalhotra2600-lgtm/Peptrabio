Product Overview

GHK-Cu is a naturally occurring tripeptide-copper complex discovered in human plasma, urine, and saliva. This remarkable compound combines the three-amino acid sequence (glycine-histidine-lysine) with a copper ion to create a stable, biologically active complex. Found throughout human tissues and biological fluids, GHK-Cu functions as a master regulator of tissue remodeling, promoting wound healing, stimulating collagen synthesis, and providing robust anti-inflammatory and antioxidant protection.

What makes GHK-Cu particularly valuable for research is its remarkable diversity of biological activities despite its simple structure. The peptide serves as a copper delivery vehicle, facilitates wound closure, triggers angiogenesis (blood vessel formation), and regulates gene expression patterns associated with tissue health and regeneration. Copper levels decrease naturally with age, explaining why tissue repair capacity diminishes over time—a finding that has made GHK-Cu a focal point of anti-aging research and regenerative medicine investigations worldwide.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is GHK-Cu blue-green in color? ▼

The characteristic blue-green color comes from the copper ion coordinated within the peptide complex. This color is a visual indicator of complex integrity—loss of color suggests dissociation of copper from the peptide. The color arises from d-d electronic transitions of the Cu²⁺ ion in its square planar coordination geometry, a standard feature of copper-peptide complexes.

What's the difference between GHK peptide and GHK-Cu complex? ▼

GHK refers to the bare tripeptide (glycine-histidine-lysine), while GHK-Cu is this peptide coordinated with a copper ion. The copper complex exhibits significantly greater biological activity than the free peptide. Copper coordination creates a stable complex with extremely high binding affinity (Ka ~10¹⁶), enables copper delivery to cells, and is essential for most of GHK-Cu's biological effects. Most research uses the copper complex due to its superior activity and stability.

Does GHK-Cu concentration change with age? ▼

Yes, significantly. GHK-Cu levels in human plasma naturally decline with age—from approximately 200 ng/ml in youth to around 80 ng/ml in older age. This age-related decrease correlates with reduced tissue repair capacity and is thought to contribute to age-related changes in skin, wound healing, and other tissue functions. This relationship makes GHK-Cu valuable for studying age-related tissue changes and investigating potential therapeutic approaches to support aging tissues.

Can I use serum-containing media with GHK-Cu? ▼

GHK-Cu can be used in serum-containing media, but certain considerations apply. Serum contains proteins and other components that may contain copper or copper-binding molecules that could affect GHK-Cu complex stability. For experiments requiring precise control of copper bioavailability, serum-free media may be preferable. If using serum, consider baseline copper measurement and potential supplementation. The stability constant of GHK-Cu (Ka ~10¹⁶) is very high, but extreme chelator concentrations could compete for copper binding.

How do I verify my GHK-Cu remains stable? ▼

The most straightforward verification is visual assessment—your GHK-Cu should maintain its blue-green color, indicating intact copper complex. Loss of color suggests copper dissociation. Functionally, compare your experimental results to historical positive controls; significant reduction in expected responses (collagen synthesis, wound contraction, antioxidant activity) suggests degradation. For long-term studies spanning months or requiring multiple batches, requesting fresh analytical testing provides definitive verification of complex integrity and copper content.

What pH range is optimal for GHK-Cu? ▼

The optimal pH range for GHK-Cu stability and biological activity is 6.0-7.5, which corresponds to physiological pH conditions. This pH range maintains the copper complex structure and supports biological activity. Strongly acidic conditions (pH <5) may cause copper dissociation, while excessive alkalinity can affect complex stability. For cell culture studies, most standard culture media (typically pH 7.2-7.4) provide appropriate conditions for GHK-Cu stability and activity.

Is GHK-Cu the same as free copper supplementation? ▼

No, GHK-Cu is fundamentally different from free copper supplementation. GHK-Cu delivers copper in a coordinated complex form that mimics physiological copper transport, is stable, and prevents free copper toxicity. Free copper is highly reactive and toxic at elevated concentrations. GHK-Cu's biological activities result from the peptide-copper complex, combining copper delivery with the peptide's own biological properties. For research investigating copper-dependent biological processes, GHK-Cu provides a more physiologically relevant and safer approach than free copper.
