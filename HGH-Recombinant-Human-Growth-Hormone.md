# HGH (Recombinant Human Growth Hormone - 191 aa)

## Product Overview

Recombinant Human Growth Hormone (somatropin, 191 amino acids) represents a synthetic duplicate of the naturally occurring growth hormone produced by the human pituitary gland. This protein hormone acts as a master regulator of growth, metabolism, and tissue maintenance throughout the lifespan. By binding directly to growth hormone receptors on target tissues, recombinant HGH initiates complex signaling cascades that influence muscle development, fat distribution, bone density, and metabolic function independent of the pituitary gland's regulatory influence.

Unlike natural pituitary growth hormone that releases in pulsatile patterns during sleep and exercise, recombinant HGH delivers continuous, non-pulsatile pharmacokinetics when administered exogenously. This distinctive delivery mechanism activates unique physiological pathways while maintaining the biochemical specificity that makes HGH fundamental to human development and aging processes. Research demonstrates that recombinant somatropin achieves therapeutic equivalence to naturally sourced growth hormone, establishing it as the benchmark for growth hormone research and clinical investigation.

The 191-amino acid polypeptide structure contains all essential domains required for complete biological activity, making it suitable for investigating fundamental growth hormone biology, receptor signaling mechanisms, tissue remodeling processes, and metabolic regulation across diverse experimental contexts.

## Detailed Molecular Characteristics

### Structure & Composition

Recombinant human growth hormone comprises 191 amino acids arranged in a specific three-dimensional configuration essential for receptor binding and biological activity. The polypeptide chain contains four alpha-helical domains that establish the characteristic GH structure, with two disulfide bonds (between cysteines 53-165 and 182-189) providing structural stability. This architecture enables precise interactions with growth hormone receptors on target tissues while maintaining resistance to proteolytic degradation and environmental denaturation.

The 191-amino acid composition results from expression in bacterial or mammalian cell systems followed by purification to homogeneity. Post-translational modifications may include methionine oxidation at the N-terminus, though modern manufacturing minimizes such modifications through optimized expression and storage protocols. The complete amino acid sequence matches native pituitary-derived growth hormone with single amino acid precision, establishing therapeutic equivalence in receptor binding kinetics and biological outcomes.

### Biochemical Properties

- **Isoelectric Point**: pH ~5.3-5.5
- **Molecular Stability**: Stable at pH 4.0-8.0; optimal pH 6.5-7.5
- **Solubility**: Water-soluble; minimal aggregation at recommended storage temperatures
- **Density**: Approximately 1.35 g/cm³ in solution
- **Osmolarity**: Varies with reconstitution volume; hypotonic, isotonic, or hypertonic solutions possible depending on application
- **Sterility**: Non-pyrogenic formulation; endotoxin levels <1 EU/mg

### Receptor Binding Characteristics

Growth hormone exerts biological effects through a single transmembrane receptor (GHR) belonging to the cytokine receptor superfamily. The GH-GHR interaction demonstrates biphasic binding kinetics, with initial high-affinity binding followed by cooperative recruitment of a second GHR molecule to form a 2:1 ligand:receptor complex. This dimeric receptor complex triggers activation of Janus kinase-2 (JAK2) and subsequent STAT5 phosphorylation, initiating intracellular signaling cascades that regulate gene transcription.

## Comprehensive Pharmacology

### Growth Hormone Direct Effects

Growth hormone acts through multiple mechanisms that can be categorized as direct GHR-mediated effects and indirect effects mediated through insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) production. Understanding this distinction proves critical for research design, as direct GH effects occur within minutes to hours, while IGF-1-mediated effects develop over hours to days.

#### Direct Effects on Skeletal Muscle

At physiological levels, growth hormone supports normal insulin signaling while promoting protein synthesis through JAK2-STAT5 activation. Muscle cells increase amino acid uptake and incorporation into contractile proteins, with concurrent reduction in protein catabolism. This anabolic environment supports muscle maintenance and modest growth rates consistent with normal aging.

At supraphysiological doses achieved through exogenous administration, growth hormone shifts metabolic partitioning dramatically. Direct GHR activation in muscle tissue impairs insulin signaling through STAT5-mediated suppression of insulin receptor substrate-1 (IRS-1) expression. This paradoxical effect decreases glucose uptake while increasing fatty acid oxidation and free fatty acid uptake. The resulting metabolic shift prioritizes fat utilization over carbohydrate metabolism, supporting lean mass preservation during caloric restriction.

A concerning consequence of supraphysiological GH levels involves abnormal lipid accumulation within myocytes, creating lipotoxic conditions that compromise mitochondrial function and insulin signaling. While adipose tissue possesses extraordinary capacity for triglyceride storage without metabolic impairment, muscle cells lack comparable buffering capacity, making lipid accumulation potentially detrimental to long-term muscle function.

#### Direct Effects on Adipose Tissue

Growth hormone fundamentally alters adipose tissue metabolism through coordinated effects on lipolysis, lipogenesis, and local hormone signaling. At all dose ranges, GH increases adipose tissue lipolysis through β2 and β3 adrenergic receptor upregulation, promoting hormone-sensitive lipase activation and triglyceride hydrolysis. Simultaneously, GH decreases lipogenic gene expression, reducing the conversion of circulating glucose and fatty acids into stored triglycerides.

Additionally, GH downregulates 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (11βHSD1) in adipose tissue, reducing local cortisol generation and thereby decreasing glucocorticoid-mediated suppression of lipolysis. The net effect involves increased serum free fatty acid concentrations that serve as metabolic fuel and lipid signaling molecules affecting distant tissues.

Intriguingly, the increase in serum free fatty acids appears to mediate GH's anabolic effects on muscle. When experimental manipulation blocks lipolysis or prevents free fatty acid elevation, GH no longer stimulates muscle protein synthesis, suggesting that lipid signaling represents a fundamental mechanism linking GH-induced lipolysis to muscle anabolism.

#### Direct Effects on Liver

Growth hormone modulates hepatic metabolism through mechanisms that support glucose homeostasis during fasting and exercise. GH stimulates hepatic glycogenolysis, promoting glucose release that maintains serum glucose during periods of negative energy balance. Simultaneously, GH impairs hepatic insulin sensitivity, reducing glucose uptake and utilization, thereby directing glucose toward peripheral tissues requiring energy.

The liver increases uptake and storage of circulating triglycerides in response to GH, with increased synthesis and secretion of VLDL cholesterol. This hepatic lipogenic response appears paradoxical given GH's lipolytic effects on adipose tissue, but reflects GH's role in supporting hepatic gluconeogenesis and supporting energy mobilization during metabolic challenges. Extended GH exposure can result in fatty liver (hepatic steatosis) and dyslipidemia, representing adverse metabolic consequences of supraphysiological GH levels.

### IGF-1 Mediated Effects

Growth hormone stimulates production of insulin-like growth factor-1 in response tissues, particularly the liver, establishing the endocrine IGF-1 axis, and in target tissues themselves, establishing autocrine and paracrine IGF-1 signaling. Circulating IGF-1 produced by the liver provides systemic anabolic effects, while locally produced IGF-1 in muscle and other tissues supports tissue-specific growth and remodeling.

#### IGF-1 Effects in Muscle

IGF-1 activates the IGF-1 receptor (IGF-1R), a receptor tyrosine kinase that initiates phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/Akt signaling and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascades. These pathways promote glucose uptake through GLUT4 translocation, activate mTORC1 signaling that drives protein synthesis, and suppress protein degradation through FOXO3 transcription factor inhibition.

Critically, IGF-1 also stimulates myogenic precursor cell proliferation and fusion, increasing the number of nuclei per muscle fiber—a process termed myonuclear accretion. This expansion of myonuclear number permits subsequent increases in protein synthetic capacity, establishing a fundamental mechanism through which growth hormone and IGF-1 exposure can produce lasting increases in muscle size potential.

#### IGF-1 Effects in Adipose Tissue

Unlike growth hormone's lipolytic effects, IGF-1 demonstrates minimal direct effects on adipose tissue lipolysis or lipogenesis. IGF-1R expression on adipocytes is limited, reducing the tissue's responsiveness to circulating IGF-1. The adipose tissue effects attributed to growth hormone reflect primarily direct GHR signaling rather than IGF-1-mediated mechanisms.

#### IGF-1 Effects in Liver

The liver produces the majority of circulating IGF-1 in response to GH stimulation, but possesses limited IGF-1R expression itself, rendering the liver relatively unresponsive to feedback inhibition by its own IGF-1 product. This architectural feature permits continued GH-stimulated IGF-1 production despite elevated circulating IGF-1 levels, supporting sustained metabolic effects during sustained GH exposure.

## Research Applications

### GH Receptor Biology Investigation

Research examining growth hormone receptor signaling explores fundamental mechanisms through which a single receptor mediates diverse physiological responses. Laboratory studies investigate the biphasic binding kinetics of GH to GHR, the formation of productive GHR dimers, and the recruitment and activation of JAK2 kinase. Subsequent research examines STAT5 phosphorylation, nuclear translocation, and DNA binding at GH-responsive promoter elements controlling growth hormone-regulated gene expression.

Advanced studies employ receptor mutagenesis to identify critical amino acid residues mediating ligand binding, dimerization, and kinase recruitment. Structure-activity relationship studies using truncated or modified GH peptides establish the minimal sequences required for receptor activation. Comparative studies across species examine evolutionary conservation of GHR signaling, providing insight into fundamental mechanisms versus species-specific adaptations.

### IGF-1 Axis Modulation

The GH-IGF-1 axis represents a central regulatory system controlling growth, metabolism, and aging. Research investigations examine hepatic IGF-1 production in response to GH stimulation, characterizing the dose-response relationship and temporal dynamics of IGF-1 synthesis and secretion. Studies measure IGF-1 bioavailability through quantification of IGF-binding proteins (IGFBPs) that sequester circulating IGF-1 and regulate tissue-level availability.

Advanced investigations examine local IGF-1 production in peripheral tissues, distinguishing endocrine effects of hepatic IGF-1 from autocrine/paracrine effects of locally produced IGF-1. Research employs tissue-specific IGF-1 knockout models and cell-autonomous IGF-1R studies to establish the relative contributions of systemic versus local IGF-1 signaling to specific growth-related outcomes.

### Body Composition Remodeling

Experiments investigating body composition changes employ in vivo models measuring muscle mass accretion, adipose tissue lipolysis, and net nitrogen retention. Whole-body composition studies employ dual-energy x-ray absorptiometry (DEXA), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), or computed tomography (CT) to quantify changes in lean mass and fat mass in response to GH administration.

Mechanistic studies examine muscle protein synthesis and breakdown rates using stable isotope tracer methodology, measuring the individual components of protein turnover. Adipose tissue lipolysis studies employ microdialysis sampling to measure interstitial glycerol and fatty acid concentrations, directly assessing tissue-level lipolytic activity. Integrated studies combine whole-body measurements with mechanistic investigations to establish how molecular-level changes translate to body composition outcomes.

### Growth Hormone Deficiency Models

Rodent models of complete GH deficiency (dwarf mice and rats with GH gene mutations or pituitary aplasia) permit investigation of GH replacement effects on growth, metabolism, and aging. These models establish baseline physiological requirements for GH and permit dose-response characterization of therapeutic GH replacement. Studies examine restoration of growth rates, metabolic normalization, and reversal of aging-related phenotypes through GH replacement.

Partial GH deficiency models, such as those achieved through targeted GH receptor knockout in specific tissues or cell types, enable investigation of tissue-specific GH requirements. These sophisticated approaches establish the relative contributions of GH effects on liver, muscle, adipose tissue, bone, and other target organs to integrated physiological outcomes.

### Pulsatile Versus Non-Pulsatile Delivery

Natural GH secretion occurs in discrete pulses, primarily during sleep and following exercise, establishing a pulsatile temporal pattern. Exogenous GH administration typically provides continuous non-pulsatile delivery, creating a distinctly different pharmacokinetic profile. Research comparing pulsatile versus continuous delivery elucidates how temporal patterns of GH exposure influence physiological outcomes.

Studies demonstrate that pulsatile GH delivery produces greater hepatic IGF-1 production and potentially different metabolic effects compared to continuous delivery at equivalent integrated doses. These findings suggest that natural GH secretion patterns evolved to optimize specific physiological responses, and that mimicking these patterns through pulsatile delivery may enhance therapeutic efficacy compared to continuous administration.

### Age-Related Growth Hormone Decline

Growth hormone production declines progressively with advancing age, contributing to age-related changes in body composition, metabolism, muscle strength, and bone density. Research investigates whether age-related GH decline represents a primary aging mechanism or an adaptive response to age-related changes in GH signaling. Studies examine whether GH replacement in aging individuals restores youthful physiology or produces adverse consequences.

Advanced research employs transcriptomic and proteomic approaches to characterize how aging alters GH signaling in target tissues, examining age-related changes in GHR expression, JAK2-STAT5 signaling, and GH-responsive gene expression. These mechanistic investigations establish whether age-related GH decline reflects reduced GH production, reduced target tissue responsiveness, or both processes operating in parallel.

## Storage, Reconstitution & Administration

### Storage of Lyophilized Powder

Unreconstituted growth hormone vials maintain stability when stored under refrigeration at 2-8°C or in a standard freezer at -20°C. Brief room temperature exposure during shipping or temporary handling produces no significant degradation. Store vials in their original containers with intact seals, protecting from light exposure through opaque packaging.

Properly stored lyophilized HGH remains stable for 24 months, maintaining ≥99% purity and full biological activity. Visual inspection provides quality verification—the powder should appear white to off-white without discoloration, cake formation, or crystallization. Discoloration suggests oxidative degradation or contamination and warrants rejection of the vial.

### Reconstitution Protocol

Growth hormone vials require reconstitution with bacteriostatic water for injection, sterile saline for injection, or sterile water for injection prior to administration. Bacteriostatic water, containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative, extends reconstituted solution stability and provides antimicrobial protection.

**Reconstitution Steps:**

1. **Aseptic Preparation**: Clean the vial rubber stopper with a 70% isopropyl alcohol wipe using firm circular motions. Allow 30 seconds for alcohol to evaporate.

2. **Solvent Addition**: Draw the predetermined reconstitution volume into an insulin syringe with a small gauge needle (23-29 gauge). Inject slowly into the vial, directing the stream toward the vial wall rather than directly onto the lyophilized powder. Forceful injection creates excessive foaming and potentially denatures the protein.

3. **Dissolution**: Gently swirl the vial for 1-2 minutes, allowing the powder to dissolve completely. A properly reconstituted solution should be clear and colorless. Do not shake vigorously—the resulting foam denatures the protein and reduces bioactivity.

4. **Verification**: The reconstituted solution should be clear and free of particles. Cloudiness, crystallization, or particulate matter indicates contamination or improper reconstitution and warrants discarding the preparation.

### Reconstitution Volume Considerations

Different reconstitution volumes offer practical advantages and disadvantages:

- **Minimal Volume (0.8-1.0 mL)**: Permits easy calculation of dosing (e.g., 1.0 mL reconstitution yields 1 IU per 0.1 mL for an 8 IU vial). However, substantial GH remains bound to vial surfaces and cannot be extracted, resulting in 10% product loss.

- **Moderate Volume (1.5-2.0 mL)**: Reduces product loss to 5-7% while maintaining reasonable injection volumes. Requires calculation of concentrations.

- **Larger Volume (2.4 mL)**: Minimizes product loss to ~4% while maintaining convenient dosing intervals (e.g., 0.3 mL per 1 IU). Larger injection volumes may be less convenient.

### Reconstituted Solution Storage

Refrigerate all reconstituted HGH at 2-8°C, maintaining sterility through aseptic technique. Solutions prepared with bacteriostatic water remain stable for 2-4 weeks with proper refrigeration. Solutions prepared with sterile water or saline, lacking antimicrobial preservation, should be used within 24-48 hours.

**Storage Best Practices:**

- Label all vials with reconstitution date and time
- Maintain vial seals to prevent contamination
- Protect from light using opaque vial storage boxes
- Avoid temperature fluctuations through consistent refrigeration
- Discard solutions showing cloudiness, crystallization, or discoloration

### Injection Administration

Injectable recombinant HGH employs subcutaneous (SC) or intramuscular (IM) routes, with insulin syringes (29-gauge needle) facilitating convenient administration. Subcutaneous injection delivers HGH into the fatty tissue layer beneath the dermis, supporting rapid absorption over 3-6 hours. Intramuscular injection deposits HGH directly into skeletal muscle, potentially accelerating absorption and more closely matching the rapid delivery of endogenous GH pulses.

**Injection Site Rotation:**

The subcutaneous tissue of the thigh, abdomen, and buttocks provides suitable injection sites. Rotating injection sites daily prevents lipoatrophy (fat tissue loss) at sites receiving repeated injections. Consistent injection site use can produce localized tissue changes, cosmetic deformities, and altered absorption kinetics.

**Administration Technique:**

1. Select injection site and clean with 70% isopropyl alcohol
2. Pinch skin to create an accessible subcutaneous pocket
3. Insert needle at 45-90 degree angle, rapidly penetrating skin
4. Inject reconstituted HGH slowly over 3-5 seconds
5. Withdraw needle and apply gentle pressure with clean gauze
6. Record injection date, time, dose, and site in treatment log

## Quality Assurance & Analytical Testing

### Purity Verification

Every batch of recombinant HGH undergoes high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis confirming ≥99% purity. The analytical method employs reversed-phase HPLC with UV detection at 214 nm and 280 nm wavelengths, detecting protein chromophores. Chromatographic separation resolves the primary HGH peak from oxidized species (methionine sulfoxide oxidation), deamidation products, and any truncated or misfolded variants.

Purity determination quantifies the percentage of authentic 191-amino acid somatropin relative to all protein-containing peaks. The requirement for ≥99% purity ensures minimal contamination by inactive variants that could confound research results or reduce therapeutic efficacy.

### Molecular Weight Confirmation

Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) confirms the molecular weight of 22,124 Da ± 2 Da, corresponding to the 191-amino acid polypeptide. Multiple charged ion species are detected (z = +15 to +25), with deconvolution to neutral mass confirming the theoretical molecular weight. MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry provides orthogonal confirmation using a different ionization methodology.

Mass spectrometry confirms not only the overall molecular weight but also the presence or absence of post-translational modifications. Oxidation of methionine residues, deamidation of asparagine and glutamine residues, and other chemical modifications produce characteristic mass shifts detectable by MS analysis.

### Protein Content Determination

Amino acid analysis (AAA) or Bradford/BCA protein assay quantifies the actual protein content relative to theoretical peptide mass. Amino acid analysis performs acid hydrolysis of the sample followed by HPLC quantification of amino acid composition. This methodology provides absolute quantification independent of protein standard reference materials.

Protein assays (Bradford or bicinchoninic acid [BCA] methods) employ colorimetric detection of protein-dye complexes, quantifying protein concentration relative to standard curves. These rapid assays support routine batch testing, while amino acid analysis provides confirmatory quantification.

### Endotoxin & Sterility Testing

Bacterial endotoxin testing employs the Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) method, detecting endotoxin contamination at levels ≥1 EU/mg. All batches test negative or at minimal levels, ensuring safety for biological research applications.

Sterility testing incubates reconstituted samples in culture media under conditions supporting growth of bacteria and fungi. Absence of microbial growth after 14 days confirms sterile manufacturing and aseptic processing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's the difference between recombinant HGH and animal-derived growth hormone?**

A: Recombinant HGH is synthesized through bacterial or mammalian cell expression of the human GH gene, producing an exact duplicate of naturally occurring growth hormone. Animal-derived growth hormone comes from pituitary tissue of slaughtered animals. Recombinant HGH offers consistent quality, purity, and safety compared to animal-derived preparations, which carry theoretical risks of prion disease transmission (though this risk is theoretical rather than documented).

**Q: Does HGH need to be kept frozen after reconstitution?**

A: No. Reconstituted HGH should be refrigerated at 2-8°C, but freezing is not necessary and can potentially reduce stability through freeze-thaw cycles. Bacteriostatic water-reconstituted solutions remain stable for 2-4 weeks with proper refrigeration.

**Q: Why does HGH work better when administered in the evening?**

A: Natural growth hormone secretion peaks during sleep, particularly during deep sleep stages. Evening administration of exogenous HGH aligns with circadian physiology and may optimize receptor signaling and downstream effects. Research suggests pulsatile (evening) delivery produces greater IGF-1 production than continuous (multiple daily) dosing at equivalent total doses.

**Q: Can HGH be mixed with other substances in the same syringe?**

A: No. HGH should be reconstituted in bacteriostatic water alone and not mixed with other medications or solutions. Combining HGH with other substances risks protein denaturation, altered pharmacokinetics, or unpredictable interactions. Each medication should be administered via separate injections.

**Q: What indicates that HGH has degraded or become contaminated?**

A: Visual inspection reveals degradation through cloudiness, discoloration, crystallization, or particulate matter in the reconstituted solution. Properly reconstituted HGH should be clear and colorless. Any deviation warrants discarding the preparation. Additionally, loss of biological activity (reduced IGF-1 response or body composition changes) suggests degradation, though this may only become apparent through research endpoints.

**Q: Are there alternatives to injected HGH?**

A: Oral growth hormone is rapidly degraded by digestive enzymes, rendering it biologically inactive. Nasal/intranasal delivery has been investigated but provides poor bioavailability. Intramuscular and subcutaneous injection remain the established routes for therapeutic HGH delivery, though inhaled and transdermal formulations are under development.

**Q: How should I calculate the correct injection volume from a reconstituted vial?**

A: Divide the total units in the vial by the reconstitution volume to determine the concentration. For example: 8 IU vial ÷ 0.8 mL = 10 IU/mL. To inject 1 IU, withdraw 0.1 mL. To inject 2 IU, withdraw 0.2 mL. Label the vial with the concentration immediately after reconstitution for accurate future dosing.
